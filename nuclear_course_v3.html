Why today matters

The issue isn't history — it's now

Most students arrive thinking nuclear war is a Cold War relic. Today, using the documentary and its context, we challenge that assumption together. By the end of Day 1 you'll understand how risk accumulates — and why your voice matters.

Today's sequence
Pre-reading — Ch. 1 of The Button by Perry & Collina
Film screening — How to Stop a Nuclear War (~90 min)
Discussion — Small group + full class on the film's arguments
Reflection — Anonymous poll + written check-in
Key concepts introduced today

Nuclear deterrence

The logic that having nukes prevents their use — and why systems failures challenge this assumption.

Risk by accident

Most close calls were errors and miscommunication, not aggression. 1983's Able Archer nearly triggered a launch.

Student agency

The 1980s freeze movement shows students have moved nuclear policy before. This course is about what comes next.

Pre-reading

"The danger is not that our leaders are evil. The danger is that the system we have built gives very little room for error — and in a crisis, error is almost guaranteed."

— William Perry & Tom Collina, The Button (2020), Ch. 1 (paraphrased for discussion)

Reading: The Button — Chapter 1

Read pages 1–18. Focus on Perry's account of the moments when he, as Secretary of Defense, came close to recommending a nuclear strike based on false information. Pay attention to: how the decision was made, who was in the room, and how much time they had.

What part of the reading surprised, interested, or confused you?

Gabor Peterdi — Nervous Lobster, 1947
Oil on canvas, 34 × 44½"
[Image shown by facilitator on screen]
Gabor Peterdi, Nervous Lobster, 1947. Oil on canvas, 34 × 44½"
Before you read anything: What do you see? What do you feel? What questions come up?

Gabor Peterdi was a Hungarian-American artist who studied in Italy before immigrating to the United States in 1939. He enlisted in the US Army and fought in Europe during World War II, then established and directed the Graphic Workshop at the Brooklyn Museum Art School. His work explores vulnerability and tension. This piece was created using etching and gaufrage (embossing on metal plates) on zinc.

Possible interpretations
  • The distorted lobster can be read as a symbol of nature's fragility under technological pressure
  • The mechanical quality reflects an unease about how technology and nature have become entangled
  • The red coloring signals anxiety about the increasing influence of technology on society
Activity 2 — Artwork 2 of 3
Dorothy Dehner — Woman #2, 1954
Watercolor and ink on paper, 22¾ × 18"
[Image shown by facilitator on screen]
Dorothy Dehner, Woman #2, 1954. Watercolor and ink on paper, 22¾ × 18"
Look first — what do you see? What feelings or questions come up before you read anything?

Dorothy Dehner was an American painter and sculptor born in 1901 in Cleveland, Ohio, known for her abstract work. After her divorce in 1950, she became more professionally active and studied printmaking at William Stanley Hayter's Atelier 17. Her work after 1950 focused on individuals' personal stories in the Atomic Age.

Possible interpretations
  • Meant to express an internal psychological impact rather than an external threat
  • Interpreted by many as a human-centered response to the Atomic Age — survival and identity, not politics
  • The fragmented, looping forms may represent the tension between individual identity and social instability
Activity 2 — Artwork 3 of 3
László Moholy-Nagy — Nuclear II, 1946
Abstract oil painting (dimensions TBC)
[Image shown by facilitator on screen]
László Moholy-Nagy, Nuclear II, 1946. Oil on canvas (dimensions to be confirmed)
Look first — what do you see? What do the shapes, colors, or composition make you feel?

László Moholy-Nagy was a Hungarian painter and photographer, heavily influenced by constructivism. He was a strong advocate for integrating technology into the arts. This abstract oil painting, composed of geometric shapes and muted colors, was made in response to the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki — while Moholy-Nagy was recovering from cancer.

Possible interpretations
  • A more structured and even optimistic reading of nuclear energy — technology as progress
  • The white squares and triangles suggest missile-like shapes beneath the geometry
  • The vibrant colors could celebrate nuclear technology as a source of renewal — but raises the question: can we separate nuclear power from nuclear weapons?

Answer individually first, then discuss with a partner before sharing with the full class.

Discussion question 1

Neither fully captures it — lived experience is irreplaceable

  • Why might media and artists portray the same event so differently?

From understanding to action

Days 1–3 built your understanding of the problem. Today we look at how change actually happens — through treaties, movements, and individual action — and begin mapping your own pathway.

Key frameworks

Treaty-based change

How international law constrains (and sometimes changes) state behavior — and who drives those treaties.

Movement-based change

From SANE to the Nuclear Freeze to ICAN — how civil society moves policy.

Individual action

Writing, organizing, voting, running for office — the concrete steps available to any student.

Primary source — TPNW

Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons (2021)

Read Articles 1–4 of the TPNW, provided by your facilitator. Focus on: what states agree to prohibit, how verification works, and which major powers have not signed.

Reading response

What surprised you about the treaty? What do you think explains why the US, Russia, and China have not signed?

Case studies — movements that moved policy

The Nuclear Freeze Movement (1980s)

One million people rallied in Central Park in 1982. Within a decade, the US and USSR had signed the INF Treaty. How did a student-led movement become a global force — and what were its limits?

ICAN's Nobel Prize campaign

The International Campaign to Abolish Nuclear Weapons won the 2017 Nobel Peace Prize three years after the TPNW negotiations began. A coalition of NGOs, not governments, drove the process. What does this tell us about where power sits?

Discussion question

Which lever for change do you find most credible after today's case studies?

International treaties and legal frameworks
Grassroots movements and public pressure
Electoral politics — voting and running for office
Media and cultural change — shifting how people think
Discussion prompts

How do these levers interact? Can one work without the others? Where do you see yourself fitting in?

Opening script (word for word)

"Before we begin, I want to name something. The topic we're exploring this week is serious — it touches on fear, uncertainty, and real global stakes. It's okay to feel uncomfortable. There's no pressure to share anything you're not ready to share. If you need to step out at any point, please do. And if something comes up for you emotionally, come find me."

Follow with: 3 minutes of quiet breathing. Ask students to put phones away and take three slow breaths with you. No discussion yet.

⚠
Film debrief — handling emotional responses
Important

After the film, pause for 2 minutes of silence before opening discussion. Do not rush to fill the quiet.

If students seem overwhelmed: "It's okay if this brought something up. You don't have to respond to everything right now. Let's take a breath together."

If a student disengages: Don't call on them. Allow written responses as an alternative to speaking. The anonymous poll is specifically designed to give quieter students (Jason-type personas) a low-stakes entry point.

If misinformation surfaces: "That's a perspective I've heard — let's look at what the film specifically says and then compare." Redirect to evidence, not correction.

💡
Discussion facilitation tips
Pedagogy

The MCQ questions are designed so students answer privately first — this gives every student an opinion before peer pressure sets in.

Recommended sequence: (1) Students answer individually, (2) Reveal class results on screen, (3) Ask: "What surprised you about the results?" before opening the floor.

Quieter students: Offer a "write first, then share" option. Ask them to write one sentence response before you call on anyone. This approach is especially effective for engineering/technical students who may feel less comfortable in debate formats.

Important

Day 3 engages with moral philosophy under conditions of mass violence. Some students may find the ethical framing destabilizing — particularly those from communities historically affected by nuclear testing (Marshall Islands, Navajo Nation, downwinders in the American West).

Before the discussion: "I want to acknowledge that for some communities, nuclear weapons are not an abstract policy question — they are lived experience. If anything today brings up something personal, you are welcome to step back or speak with me privately."

💡
Discussion tips — deterrence ethics

The deterrence ethics question is intentionally open — there is no single correct answer. Resist the urge to resolve the tension. Your role is to keep the conversation evidence-based and ensure no position is mocked.

If a student says "this is just how it is": "What would it take for that to change? Has it ever changed in history?"

If a student expresses hopelessness: "Let's hold that feeling — we're going to look at Day 4 specifically for evidence that change is possible."
