Day 1 — Introduction & film connection
4 activities Est. 5 hrs No prior knowledge needed
👤
Facilitator note — Marcus & Dr. Rodriguez
Open with the grounding exercise (3 min). Remind students that opt-out is always available. If the room feels tense after the film, use the de-escalation script in your facilitator guide before moving to discussion.
Why this module

The issue isn't history — it's now

Students often enter thinking nuclear war is a Cold War relic. This module uses the film alongside present-day examples to challenge that. By the end of Day 1, you'll understand how risk actually accumulates — and why individual action matters.

Today's sequence
Grounding exercise — 3 min breathing + opt-out reminder
Pre-reading — Ch. 1 of The Button by Perry & Collina
Film screening — How to Stop a Nuclear War (~90 min)
Lesson + reflection — Key concepts, film analysis, check-in poll
Key concepts

Nuclear deterrence

The logic that possessing nukes prevents their use — and the systems failures that challenge it.

Risk by accident

Most close calls weren't from aggression — they were errors, miscommunication, and system failures under pressure.

Student agency

The 1980s freeze movement shows students have moved nuclear policy before. You can too.

Lesson — The logic (and limits) of deterrence
Visual Discussion
Reading excerpt

"The danger is not that our leaders are evil. The danger is that the system we have built gives very little room for error — and in a crisis, error is almost guaranteed."

— William Perry & Tom Collina, The Button (2020), Ch. 1 (paraphrased for discussion)

Film reference
How to Stop a Nuclear War — 00:18:40 Able Archer 1983: how a NATO war exercise nearly triggered a Soviet nuclear response.
👤
Facilitator note
Pause the film here and ask: "What surprised you?" — a low-stakes, open-ended entry point. Quieter students can write first before sharing. No debate required at this stage.
Framing — as you watched

The film argues that nuclear risk is not primarily about intent — it is about structure, miscommunication, and time pressure under stress. The Able Archer segment shows how a routine exercise was nearly catastrophic. This is a systems problem, not just a political one.

Discussion question
For small groups or written response

The film suggests the greatest danger isn't aggression — it's accident. Do you agree? What systemic change — policy, technology, or diplomacy — would address the specific risks it describes? Use at least one example from the documentary.

Day 1 reflection & check-in
Ungraded · ~8 min · anonymous option available
♡
Emotional check-in: Before you start — how are you feeling after the film? It's okay if this content brought up strong reactions. Take a breath. You can skip any question and return to it later.
Quick poll — share your reaction (anonymous)
After watching the film, which of these best describes how you feel?
Motivated to learn more and take action
Surprised — I didn't know the risk was this current
Overwhelmed — this is a lot to take in
Skeptical — I want to see more evidence
Question 1 — comprehension
According to the film, what is the primary source of nuclear risk today?
Deliberate first strikes by hostile states
Accidental launches or miscommunication under pressure
Terrorist acquisition of warheads
Failure of international inspections
Question 2 — reflection
In 2–3 sentences: what's one thing from the film that surprised you — and why does it matter to you personally?

Syllabus summary

Graded work

Daily reflections (ungraded), one short essay (Day 3), and a final action brief presented on Day 5.

Final project

A 2-page civic action brief: one concrete step you, your campus, or your community could take — backed by evidence from the course.
